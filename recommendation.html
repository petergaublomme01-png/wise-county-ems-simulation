Section 1
Executive Summary

Wise County operates 8,938 emergency calls annually across a mountainous 403-square-mile service area. The current two-unit deployment leaves the Wise zone uncovered during peak hours, exposing roughly 2,970 residents to response times exceeding the NFPA 1710 eight-minute ALS target.

This analysis applies an Erlang-C queuing model to four deployment scenarios under five road conditions and two day-type multipliers. The recommended daytime configuration — three units staged at Wise, Norton, and Big Stone Gap — achieves 87.4% coverage within eight minutes and satisfies the twelve-minute absolute constraint for all zones under normal and rain conditions. Overnight operations may safely revert to the two-unit Norton + BSG configuration given reduced 15% call volume, maintaining full twelve-minute compliance with 74% eight-minute coverage.

Bottom line: Deploy a third unit to Wise Rescue Squad during 07:00–23:00. Maintain Norton + BSG overnight. During snow/ice events, pre-position the third unit to Norton to reduce corridor response times.
Section 2
Problem Statement

Wise County Emergency Medical Services faces three simultaneous pressures:

  • Geography: The county spans rugged Appalachian terrain with two-lane mountain roads, switchbacks, and seasonal ice accumulation that inflate travel times by 22–56% versus fair-weather estimates.
  • Demand growth: Call volume has grown 6.2% over five years, driven by an aging population and opioid-crisis-related incidents concentrated in the BSG–Norton corridor.
  • Staffing constraints: With 19 career and 40 volunteer personnel, simultaneous calls reduce effective unit availability below the theoretical fleet size.

The core optimization question is: Given a fixed budget allowing two or three deployable units, where should they be staged to minimize worst-case response time while maximizing the population fraction reached within eight minutes?

Section 4
Mathematical Model

Erlang-C queuing model

We model the EMS system as an M/M/c queue (Poisson arrivals, exponential service, c servers) to estimate unit utilization and the probability that all units are busy when a call arrives.

ρ = λ × T_s / (c × 24×60) where: λ = adjusted calls per day = base_λ × cond_mult × day_mult T_s = average service time = 92.8 minutes c = number of deployed units Traffic intensity: a = ρ × c Erlang-C formula (probability all servers busy): (a^c / c!) × 1/(1-ρ) C(c,ρ) = ───────────────────────────────────────── (a^c / c!) × 1/(1-ρ) + Σ_{k=0}^{c-1} a^k/k!

Coverage metrics

Worst-case RT = max over all zones of: min travel time from any staged unit to that zone + 2 min dispatch overhead 8-min coverage % = Σ (population_z × I[nearest_unit_TT_z ≤ 8]) / total_population where I[·] is 1 if the indicator condition holds, 0 otherwise.

Feasibility criteria

  • FEASIBLE: Worst-case RT ≤ 12 min AND 8-min coverage ≥ 80%
  • MARGINAL: Worst-case RT ≤ 12 min AND 8-min coverage < 80%
  • INFEASIBLE: Worst-case RT > 12 min (violates hard constraint)

The 12-minute threshold is the NFPA 1710 absolute maximum for ALS response. The 80% eight-minute target aligns with NFPA 1710’s 90th-percentile recommendation adjusted for rural/suburban service areas.

Section 7
Optimal Deployment Recommendation
✅ Recommended configuration: Three-unit daytime deployment — Wise Rescue Squad, Norton Rescue Squad, Big Stone Gap Rescue Squad — operating 07:00–23:00.

Why this configuration?

  • Reduces worst-case response time from 23 min (Wise zone, baseline) to 11 min.
  • Increases 8-min population coverage from 65% to 87.4% under normal conditions.
  • Unit utilization of ρ = 0.524 leaves adequate spare capacity for simultaneous calls.
  • Remains FEASIBLE under rain and peak-hour conditions; becomes MARGINAL only under snow.

Overnight (23:00–07:00): Revert to Norton + BSG only. Call volume drops 15%; worst-case RT for the Wise zone rises to 11 min (within the 12-min constraint). The 8-min coverage falls to 74%, which is MARGINAL but operationally justified given cost and staffing constraints at night.

Snow/ice protocol: When VDOT issues a Level 2 or higher road warning, pre-position the third unit to Norton rather than Wise. This reduces the critical Norton–BSG corridor travel time and limits snow-adjusted worst-case RT to 19 min (from 22 min with Wise staging). Additional mutual-aid activation from Lee County is recommended.

⚠️ Limitation: This model treats each unit as always available from its staging point. Real-world dispatch to a scene renders that unit temporarily unavailable. See Section 10 for full discussion.
Section 8
Sensitivity Analysis

Three parameters most significantly affect the recommendation:

1. Call volume (λ)
A 20% increase in calls (to 29.4/day) raises utilization to ρ = 0.628 for the three-unit daytime config. The queue remains stable and the FEASIBLE classification holds. A 40% surge (34.3/day) pushes ρ to 0.732 — still stable but P(all busy) rises to 18%, indicating four units would be warranted.

2. Service time (Ts)
If average busy time increases from 92.8 to 120 min (e.g., due to hospital diversion to Kingsport), ρ rises to 0.677 for three units. Still FEASIBLE under normal conditions, but snow-adjusted utilization reaches 1.056 — technically unstable. Protocol: activate standby volunteer unit when Holston Valley diversion is in effect.

3. Road condition
Snow/ice is the single largest risk factor, multiplying effective arrival rate by 1.56×. Under these conditions the three-unit config becomes MARGINAL (8-min coverage drops to 71%) and overnight two-unit becomes INFEASIBLE (worst RT = 19 min). Mutual-aid pre-activation thresholds are recommended at NWS Winter Storm Watch issuance.

Section 10
Limitations & Assumptions
  • Static staging: The model assumes each unit is at its staging location when dispatched. In reality, a unit responding to a call is unavailable, and the effective coverage degrades dynamically. A hypercube queuing model would more precisely capture this, at the cost of significantly greater complexity.
  • Exponential service times: The Erlang-C model assumes call service times are exponentially distributed. EMS service times are empirically log-normal; using the exponential approximation slightly overestimates queue congestion at high utilization.
  • Homogeneous demand: Call arrival rate is treated as uniform across the day. Actual peak hours (06:00–10:00, 18:00–22:00) have 40–60% higher demand. A time-varying Poisson model would improve overnight estimates.
  • Road network approximation: Travel times are estimated from OpenRouteService driving-car profiles. Emergency vehicle routing (lights and siren) can reduce travel time by 10–20% on US-23; this is not modeled.
  • Mutual-aid excluded: Lee County and Scott County mutual-aid agreements are not modeled. Including them would improve worst-case RT estimates for the BSG and southern corridor zones.
Section 11
Conclusion

The Erlang-C analysis demonstrates that adding a third EMS unit to the Wise staging point during daytime hours substantially improves service equity for the 2,970 residents currently underserved by the Norton-only northern coverage zone. The daytime three-unit configuration is the only scenario that simultaneously satisfies the twelve-minute hard constraint and achieves at least eighty percent eight-minute coverage under normal and rain conditions.

The overnight two-unit configuration is operationally acceptable given reduced demand, provided that snow/ice pre-position protocols are in place and mutual-aid thresholds are defined clearly in the dispatch protocol. A six-month post-implementation review using actual CAD timestamps will allow the model to be recalibrated with empirical rather than estimated service times.

The simulation tool accompanying this report allows decision-makers to interactively explore how changes in fleet size, staging location, road condition, and time of day affect coverage metrics — providing a durable planning resource beyond this competition cycle.
